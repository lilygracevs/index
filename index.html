Have You Ever Wondered What It's Like in Outer Space?



Outer space is the expanse that extends beyond Earth's atmosphere. There is not a definite point where our atmosphere ends and outer space begins, but the Karman line, at an altitude of 62 miles above sea level, is usually used as the dividing line.



What Would You Feel?

There is almost no gravity and no friction. This allows the moon, stars, and planets to travel freely on their orbits.


It might sound nice to float around in space with no gravity, but it's also very cold.
The baseline temperature is 2.7 kelvins, or -454.81 F!



Fun Fact The hottest planet in our solar system is Venus, which can reach 842 degrees F.

Have you ever wondered why astronauts wear suits in space? Well, when you're up that high, the atmospheric pressure matches the vapor pressure of water at the temperature of the human body. This causes fluids in the throat and lungs boil away. Yikes!

The drop in pressure also causes ebullism, which is a condition where gas bubbles form in the bodily fluids. This can cause the body to swell up to twice its normal size. The pressure inside the suit prevents this, as well as supplies oxygen to breathe.

What Would You See?

If you look down at Earth when it is night, you will see city lights shining brightly. If you look up, you will see a huge expanse filled with stars. Depending on where you are, you might also be able to see planets, which appear like large stars from a distance. Other objects you can see in the sky are nebulas, star systems, planetary rings, and other planet systems.

What Would You Hear?

Space is a vacuum, meaning sound waves aren't carried like they are here on earth. So technically, you wouldn't be able to hear anything. However, satellites traveling through space have captured some interesting radio emissions coming from space objects, which are converted into sound waves to create some interesting noises.

How Big is Outer Space?

The universe is constantly expanding. There are several different tools that sceintists use to measure the rate of expansion, but different parts of the universe appear to be expanding at different rates. According to some measurements, the observable universe is a sphere that is 28 billion light-years in diameter. But that is only what we can see. The entire universe is likely more than 7 trillion light-years across.
